PROJECT 01 / STRUCTURAL DESIGN

Lightweight aircraft-style structural bracket

FEA-driven design validated on an Instron rig: predictions landed within 4% of the physical test.

The brief was a bracket meeting stiffness targets of 3.0 kN in tension and 4.0 kN in compression at the lowest possible mass. I modelled it in Inventor, iterated the geometry through ANSYS FEA checked against hand calculations, then programmed the CNC toolpaths and machined it from polycarbonate sheet. On the Instron, the finished part carried 3.2 kN in tension, landed within half a percent of the 4.0 kN compression target, and never failed: the test topped out at its 10 kN limit with the bracket still intact.

PROJECT 02 / MECHANISM DESIGN

Multi-mechanism ball-transport device

A scotch yoke and gear-driven lift designed to a 5.0 second spec. On demo day: exactly 5.0 seconds.

The device had to move a ball through a sequence of mechanisms and deliver it at precisely 5.0 seconds. Our design used a scotch yoke to push the ball out and a gear train driving a large wheel that carried it up. I tuned the geometry in Autodesk Inventor's dynamic simulation until the model hit the target, and we prototyped through 3D printing and laser cutting. On demo day the machine delivered the ball at exactly 5.0 seconds, matching its own simulation.

PROJECT 03 / MACHINE DESIGN

Gear reducer drawing pack

Sixteen A3 sheets taking a motor-driven reducer from brief to manufacturing-ready, as a team of three.

The second MECHENG 236 project: nothing physical built, everything specified. Working as a team of three, we documented the complete system, from the motor, disk clutch and disk brake through the gearbox internals to the baseplate, each assembly with its own bill of materials. The brief split the drawing work deliberately, requiring every member to produce at least one shaft assembly drawing and one shaft component drawing. Mine were the input side: the input shaft assembly, the input shaft itself, and its M2B14 pinion, specified down to bearing fits, running tolerances and key clearances, so the sheets could go straight to a machinist. My teammates’ sheets covered the output side and shaft adapters, the lay shaft, and the housing and baseplate, and full credit to them for that work. The full system layout that closes the pack was the sheet we produced together, pulling every subassembly onto one baseplate.

PROJECT 04 / DESIGN & MANUFACTURE

Injection-moulded silicone phone case

A moulded part, a damaged lip, and the design-for-manufacture lesson that came with them.

A protective silicone case for the iPhone XR, designed for outdoor users, moulded in one shot from 3D-printed tooling. The case fit the phone and met its lightweight spec at 45.6 g against a 50 g limit, but de-moulding damaged the retaining lip, which cost it the vertical retention test: a first-hand lesson that the de-moulding process deserves as much design attention as the part itself. The failed test taught me more about designing for manufacture than a clean pass would have.

PROJECT 05 / MANUFACTURING

Welded, turned and milled: a noughts and crosses set

One small object, three machining processes, from the course where I learned to run the machines.

MECHENG 299 opened with an induction into 3D printing and laser cutting, assembling a small battery-powered motor from supplied files and finishing its base and fittings with hand tools, then moved onto the machines. The piece that sums it up is a noughts and crosses set I still own: the board is two plates I welded together, the noughts were turned on a lathe, and the crosses were milled. Those skills carried straight into prototyping the task robot and machining the structural bracket.

PROJECT 06 / MECHATRONICS

Arduino-driven task robot

Design lead (CAD) for a team robot built to collect two pods and deliver them to a drop zone, against the clock.

The task: start anywhere on the course, collect two pods, and deliver them into a drop zone as fast as possible. As design lead I designed the laser-cut and 3D-printed body and the pick-up and release mechanisms, and wrote the control code. The plan was closed-loop control with encoders; late wiring and integration problems meant the encoders were unusable on the day, so we shipped open-loop control tuned by trial and error, and the robot ran its task. It taught me to integrate the full system early and to always keep a fallback you can actually ship.
