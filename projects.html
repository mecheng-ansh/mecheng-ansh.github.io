PROJECT 01 / STRUCTURAL DESIGN

Lightweight aircraft-style structural bracket

FEA-driven design validated on an Instron rig: predictions landed within 4% of the physical test.

The brief was a bracket meeting stiffness targets of 3.0 kN in tension and 4.0 kN in compression at the lowest possible mass. I modelled it in Inventor, iterated the geometry through ANSYS FEA checked against hand calculations, then programmed the CNC toolpaths and machined it from polycarbonate sheet. On the Instron, the finished part carried 3.2 kN in tension, landed within half a percent of the 4.0 kN compression target, and never failed: the test topped out at its 10 kN limit with the bracket still intact.

PROJECT 02 / MECHANISM DESIGN

Multi-mechanism ball-transport device

A gear-driven lifting wheel and a scotch yoke ejector, built to deliver a 9.5 mm steel ball at exactly 5.0 seconds.

The brief: move a 9.5 mm steel ball through a 100 mm acrylic cube and deliver it at exactly 5.0 seconds, driven by a constant 60 rpm input. Our design lifts the ball on a gear-driven wheel working on a ferris wheel principle, then a scotch yoke ejects it through the exit hole, both running off a single six-gear train that slows the wheel to 13.29 rpm and spins the yoke disk to 105 rpm. We tuned the timing in Autodesk Inventor's dynamic simulation until the model delivered at exactly 5.0 seconds, and prototyped entirely by FDM 3D printing across five full print iterations. The physical machine ran between 4.5 and 6.0 seconds over its three demonstration runs, and chasing that gap taught us more about tolerance stack-up and friction than the clean simulation did. We also wrote it a proper flat pack assembly manual: one Allen key, eleven bolts, no glue, and you can download it as a PDF. Of the two clips, the first is the simulation at true speed and the second is the full hand cranked run at 5.1x speed.

PROJECT 03 / MACHINE DESIGN

Gear reducer drawing pack

Sixteen A3 sheets taking a motor-driven reducer from brief to manufacturing-ready, as a team of three.

The second MECHENG 236 project: nothing physical built, everything specified. Working as a team of three, we documented the complete system, from the motor, disk clutch and disk brake through the gearbox internals to the baseplate, each assembly with its own bill of materials. The brief split the drawing work deliberately, requiring every member to produce at least one shaft assembly drawing and one shaft component drawing. Mine were the input side: the input shaft assembly, the input shaft itself, and its M2B14 pinion, specified down to bearing fits, running tolerances and key clearances, so the sheets could go straight to a machinist. My teammates’ sheets covered the output side and shaft adapters, the lay shaft, and the housing and baseplate, and full credit to them for that work. The full system layout that closes the pack was the sheet we produced together, pulling every subassembly onto one baseplate.

PROJECT 04 / DESIGN & MANUFACTURE

Injection-moulded silicone phone case

A moulded part, a damaged lip, and the design-for-manufacture lesson that came with them.

A protective silicone case for the iPhone XR, designed for outdoor users, moulded in one shot from 3D-printed tooling. The case fit the phone and met its lightweight spec at 45.6 g against a 50 g limit, but de-moulding damaged the retaining lip, which cost it the vertical retention test: a first-hand lesson that the de-moulding process deserves as much design attention as the part itself. The failed test taught me more about designing for manufacture than a clean pass would have. The top slides run the physical story: the prototype on the phone, the mould open and then closed in hand, and the lip torn in de-moulding. The frame below is the paper trail: the engineering drawing, the CAD model, and the workbook sketch it all started from.

PROJECT 05 / MECHATRONICS

Arduino-driven task robot

Software lead for a team robot built to collect two pods and deliver them to a drop zone, against the clock.

The task: start anywhere on the course, collect two pods, and deliver them into a drop zone as fast as possible. I helped shape the early CAD concept for the laser-cut and 3D-printed body, then led the software: I wrote the Arduino control code and built a closed-loop control system around wheel encoders mounted parallel to the drive wheels. Late wiring and integration problems meant the encoders were unusable on the day, so the final run went open-loop, tuned by trial and error, and the robot ran its task. The clip shows a full run during testing, sped up 2x: pod off the stand, across the course, into the drop zone. No footage was taken on demo day itself. It taught me to integrate the full system early and to always keep a fallback you can actually ship.

PROJECT 06 / MANUFACTURING

Welded, turned and milled: a noughts and crosses set

One small object, three machining processes, from the course where I learned to run the machines.

MECHENG 299 opened with an induction into 3D printing and laser cutting, assembling a small battery-powered motor from supplied files: a laser cut acrylic case, a folded aluminium base finished with hand tools, a PCB I soldered every component onto, wiring I crimped and soldered from scratch, and a magnet and coil doing the driving. Then the course moved onto the machines. The piece that sums it up is a noughts and crosses set I still own: the board is two plates I welded together, the noughts were turned on a lathe, and the crosses were milled. Those skills built the manufacturing fundamentals I've leaned on in every design and manufacture paper since, from prototyping the task robot to machining the structural bracket. The first slides are the set; the second run is the motor, right down to the wiring inside its base.
